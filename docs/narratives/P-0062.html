Earl Wayne Reed Sr

1899 — 1974

Earl Wayne Reed was born on 19 July 1899 in Guthrie County, in the rolling farm country of west-central Iowa, the eldest son of John Foulk Reed and Estelle Gertrude Lambert [1][2]. He arrived in the last summer of the nineteenth century, into a young nation still catching its breath after the Panic of 1893 and the brief, feverish Spanish-American War — a country whose center of gravity was exactly the kind of small county-seat farming town, Guthrie Center, where the 1900 census found him as a one-year-old in his parents' household [2]. The Reeds were restless, as so many plains families were in those years; over the next two decades the household drifted north and west across Iowa, into South Dakota, and on toward Wisconsin, chasing the work and land that the railroads had thrown open across the upper Midwest.

His was a large and not always lucky family. Among his siblings was a brother, Harold Merle Reed, whose life ended far from any Iowa cornfield — he died in 1921 in Panama, where the recently opened Canal Zone drew a generation of young American men into its orbit of labor, soldiering, and disease [3]. The fuller shape of the family — four Reed children and, after their mother's later remarriage, an Eichinger half-brother — is set out in the dossier for Estelle herself [[P-0036]]. Earl came of age just as the United States entered the First World War; in 1917 or 1918 he registered for the draft not in Iowa but in Waukesha, Wisconsin, a marker of how far the family had already roamed by the time he reached twenty [4].

The 1920s carried Earl, like hundreds of thousands of Midwesterners, into the gravitational pull of Chicago. By 1930 he had settled in Cicero — the gritty, factory-laced industrial suburb on the city's western edge, then notorious as the staging ground of the Capone outfit — where he raised his family with his wife, Isabelle Zika, the daughter of Chicago's large Bohemian Czech community [5]. Their sons arrived through the heart of the Great Depression: Earl Jr. in December 1930, John Ronald in 1934, and James Gary in 1939 [6][7][8]. Through it all Earl worked as a carpenter [13] — a trade that, even in lean years, kept a man employed in a metropolis perpetually building, tearing down, and building again. The family stayed rooted in Cicero across both the 1930 and 1940 censuses [5][9], weathering the decade that broke so many.

When the country went to war again, Earl — by then past forty — registered in the 1942 "old man's registration," the WWII draft sweep of older men meant to inventory the nation's manpower and skills [10]. The postwar years brought the upward, outward mobility that defined his generation's later life. By the 1950 census the family had moved from Cicero into Chicago proper [11], and in the decades that followed Earl followed the great suburban tide still farther west, out past the city limits into DuPage County. He spent his last years at 4 N 711 Medinah Road in Addison [14], in the booming postwar suburbs that cornfields were rapidly becoming.

Earl Wayne Reed died on 7 April 1974, at Elk Grove Village in Cook County, Illinois, at the age of seventy-four [12]. He was buried four days later, on 11 April, at Lakewood Memorial Park in Elgin, his funeral handled by the Martin Funeral Home; the informant on his death record was a "John Reed," very likely his son John Ronald [15]. He had lived three-quarters of a century that traced the whole arc of the American twentieth century — from a horse-and-buggy Iowa farm town at the turn of the century, through the industrial boom and bust of Chicago, to a postwar suburb of expressways and subdivisions. His wife Isabelle and three sons survived him, and his namesake, Earl Wayne Reed Jr., would carry the name another fifty years.
